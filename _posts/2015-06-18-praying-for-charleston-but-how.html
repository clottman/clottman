After last night's racially-motivated mass shooting in a church in Charleston, my prayers exist as a sigh too deep for words. All I feel able to say is "Lord, have mercy. Christ, have mercy." Might there be more?

The Bible offers another medium for bringing our distress to God, the act of lamentation. In lament, we cry out to God for justice, for peace. In lament we ask God "Why? Why don't you do something about this?"

God’s creation is good, but our world is also not the way it’s supposed to be. God’s good creation has been corrupted by evil, and until Christ comes to set things right, we cry out in lament to God and move in compassion toward our neighbor; suffering and tears and evil are not God’s final intentions.

J. Todd Billings

The Psalms of lament offer a model for what healthy lamentation might look like. The people who wrote the Psalms of lament were angry, and hurt, and scared. Not unlike many people today, waking up to news of another act of terror on American soil. They asked God, "Why have you forsaken me? Why don't you do something?" God had delivered their ancestors from oppression -- where was God now?

In you our ancestors put their trust;

    they trusted and you delivered them.

To you they cried out and were saved;

    in you they trusted and were not put to shame.

Psalm 22

The Psalms may have been written about a particular time and place, but some of their descriptions of injustice feel mighty relevant to the world today.

Why, Lord, do you stand far off?

    Why do you hide yourself in times of trouble?

In his arrogance the wicked man hunts down the weak,

    who are caught in the schemes he devises.

Psalm 10

The Psalms call God to action, to bring about the justice God's people have been promised.

Awake, Lord! Why do you sleep?

    Rouse yourself! Do not reject us forever.

Why do you hide your face

    and forget our misery and oppression?

We are brought down to the dust;

    our bodies cling to the ground.

Rise up and help us;

    rescue us because of your unfailing love.

Psalm 44

And then, suddenly, the prayers of a people in desperation turn to hope. The people of God trust in God's goodness enough to offer prayers of praise and thanksgiving for God's presence, even before any worldly change has happened.

You, Lord, hear the desire of the afflicted;

    you encourage them, and you listen to their cry,

defending the fatherless and the oppressed,

    so that mere earthly mortals

    will never again strike terror.

Psalm 10

It seems a little crazy- why give thanks to a God who seems to just stand by as innocent people are slaughtered in their house of worship? And yet, all but one of the Psalms of lament ends this way. Even in the depths of our anguish, when the kingdom of God seems very, very far away, we can trust in a God who promises resurrection.

 

I encourage you today to pray one of the Psalms of lament. If the Charleston shooting weighs heavy on your mind, I found Psalm 10 to be especially apt. Bring your pain to God, uncensored. Believe me, He's heard it all before. Don't be afraid to demand justice. We can do so in the trust that the evil in the world today will not have the final say. Let the words of the Psalm transform your prayer. End with the hope that, though the pain is still very fresh, God is with us.

 

Lord, have mercy. Christ, have mercy.
